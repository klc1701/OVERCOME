THE LIBERATOR

1831-1865

William Llyod Garrison founded The Liberator, an anti-slavery newspaper in which he spoke out against slavery and for the betterment of the lives of black Americans. Garrsion soon gained the reputation as a radical abolitionist for his passionate advocacy of the immediate emancipation of slaves.

SENECA FALLS CONVENTION

1848

Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Lucretia Mott called a convention at Seneca Falls, New York to adopt the Declaration of Sentiments, modeled after the Declaration of Independence. The document called for a wide range of women's rights and the women attended numerous subsequent conventions to improve women's rights.

CIVIL RIGHTS ACT

1875

The Civil Rights Act of 1875 protexted every natural-born American citizens' human and civil rights, regardless of race, gender, or ethnicity. Under this act, newly freed black Americans were granted citizenship and was used to overturn the Black Codes in place in the South.

19TH AMENDMENT

1920

The Ninteenth Amendment guaranteed white women's right to vote. The call for suffrage began as early 1800, which involved several of generations of women banding together to organize, picket, and protest for the vote.

BROWN v. BOE

1954

Under the Warren court, the ruling of Plessey v. Ferguson, which upheld racial segregation, was overturned. In a unanimous decision, the policy of having separate but equal educational faciliites for white and black children was deemed unconstitutional, and public schools were ordered to integrate.

STONEWALL RIOTS

1969

The Stonewall Inn, a mafia-owned speakeasy-styled bar that catered to members of the gay community, was raided by the police department, who were not expecting the crowd to fight back. The raid was followed by several days of demonstrations, which are regarded as the beginnings of the modern fight for LGBTQ+ rights.

ROE v. WADE

1973

In a 7-2 decision, the Supreme Court ruled that women have the right to abortion under the right to privacy, protected by the 14th Amendment. The Court decided women have complete autonomy during the first trimester and defined state interests in the following two trimesters.

GAY AND LESBIAN RIGHTS MARCH ON WASHINGTON

1979

The National March on Washington, inspired by the assassination of openly gay politician Harvey Milk, drew thousands of supporters of LGBT rights, nationalizing the movement. The march supported issues like the ban on discrimination in the federal government based on sexual orientation.

SANDRA DAY O'CONNOR

1981

Nominated by President Ronald Reagan, Sandra Day O'Connor became the first woman to serve on the Supreme Court. The Senate voted unaminously on her nomination, and O'Connor remained a justice until 2005, when she announced her retirement.

LAWRENCE v. TEXAS

2003

John Lawrence and Tyron Garner were arrested and convicted for deviate sexual misconduct, a violation of a Texas statute that made it illegal for people of the same sex to engage in sexual conduct. The Kennedy Court, on which Sandra Day O'Connor served, ruled that the statute was in violation of the Due Process Clause.

#blacklivesmatter

2012

The movement, created after George Zimmerman was acquitted for the murder of Trayvon Martin, a black teenager, is a call to action that extends beyond the killings of black people by the police. Participants in the movement have demonstrated agains the deaths of numerous African Americans and are responsible for bringing the crimes against black Americans to national attention.

TRANSGENDER MILITARY BAN LIFTED

2016

Secretary of Defense Ash Carter announced that the ban that kept transgender individuals from openly serving in the armed forces is lifted. The lift is another step for the LGBTQ+ community in recognition by the US military, following the repeal of the "Dont' Ask, Don't Tell" policy in 2010 under President Obama.
